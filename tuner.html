Methods

We use the YIN algorithm to find the pitch of input sound.
The input signal, obtained from the Web Audio API, comes in as a discrete time-series array of amplitudes. We use the following autocorrelation function to quantify the similarity between a signal and itself delayed by some lag \(\tau\). $$ r_{t} ( \tau ) = \sum_{j = t + 1}^{t + W - \tau} x_{j} x_{j + \tau}$$ Here, \(r_t(\tau)\) is the autocorrelation function at the time intex \(t\), with a lag of \(\tau\). \(W\) is the integration window size, or the time frame in consideration. We subtract \(\tau\) from the upper summation bound to lessen the effect of larger lag sizes, with the assumption that the fundemental frequency is closer to the origin point \(t\).

The Weiner-Khinchin theorem (in the discrete time case) allows us to compute the autocorrelation funtion using a Fourier Transform.
To get to this new and convenient definition of the autocorrelation function, we can start by taking the Fourier transform \(\mathcal{F}\{r(\tau)\}(\xi)\) of \(r(\tau)\). We will use the continuous forms of functions to show these conversions.
A continuous version of our autocorrelation is $$ r(\tau) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x(t)x(t + \tau)dt $$ and its Fourier transform is $$ \mathcal{F}\{r(\tau)\}(\xi) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty}r(\tau)e^{-i2\pi\xi\tau}d\tau $$ Let's substitue in \(r(\tau)\) and swap the order of integration by Fubini's theorem. $$ \begin{align*} \mathcal{F}\{r(\tau)\}(\xi) &= \int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\left[\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x(t)x(t + \tau)dt\right]e^{-i2\pi\xi\tau}d\tau \\ &= \int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x(t)x(t + \tau)e^{-i2\pi\xi\tau}d\tau dt \\ &= \int_{-\infty}^{\infty}x(t)\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x(t + \tau)e^{-i2\pi\xi\tau}d\tau dt \end{align*} $$ If we define and substitue in \(u=t+\tau\), we can see that the inner integral can be represented as a Fourier transform of \(x\) multiplied by an exponent. $$ \begin{align*} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x(t + \tau)e^{-i2\pi\xi\tau}d\tau &= \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x(u)e^{-i2\pi\xi(u-t)}du \\ &= e^{i2\pi\xi t}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x(u)e^{-i2\pi\xi u}du \\ &= e^{i2\pi\xi t}\mathcal{F}\{x\}(\xi) \end{align*} $$ Subsituting this back in, we get $$ \begin{align*} \mathcal{F}\{r(\tau)\}(\xi) &= \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x(t)e^{i2\pi\xi t}\mathcal{F}\{x\}(\xi)dt \\ &= \mathcal{F}\{x\}(\xi)\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x(t)e^{i2\pi\xi t}dt \end{align*} $$ We may notice that $$ \int_{-\infty}^{\infty}x(t)e^{i2\pi\xi t}dt = \mathcal{F}\{x\}(-\xi) $$ giving us $$ \mathcal{F}\{r(\tau)\}(\xi) = \mathcal{F}\{x\}(\xi) \mathcal{F}\{x\}(-\xi) $$ And because for real \(x\), the Fourier transform at a negated frequency is the complex conjugate of the Fourier transform at the non-negated frequency, we get $$ \mathcal{F}\{x\}(-\xi) = \overline{\mathcal{F}\{x\}(\xi)} $$ Therefore, $$ \begin{align*} \mathcal{F}\{r(\tau)\}(\xi) &= \mathcal{F}\{x\}(\xi) \mathcal{F}\{x\}(-\xi) \\ &= \mathcal{F}\{x\}(\xi) \overline{\mathcal{F}\{x\}(\xi)} \\ &= | \mathcal{F}\{x\}(\xi) |^2 \end{align*} $$ Using the inverse Fourier transform, we can get the autocorrelation back out, this time with a formulation faster to compute. $$ \begin{align*} r(\tau) &= \mathcal{F}^{-1}\{r(\tau)\}(\mathcal{F}\{r(\tau)\}(\xi)) \\ &= \mathcal{F}^{-1}\{r(\tau)\}(| \mathcal{F}\{x\}(\xi) |^2) \end{align*} $$ Consider this difference function, used to find an unknown period: $$ d_t(\tau) = \sum_{j=1}^{W}(x_j - x_{j-\tau})^2 $$ When \(d(\tau)=0\), we have a tau which is a multiple of the period.

This can be expanded, $$ d_t(\tau) = \sum_{j=1}^{W}(x_j^2 + x_{j-\tau}^2 -2x_jx_{j-\tau})^2 $$ and we can substitute in the autocorrelation function: $$ d_t(\tau) = r_t(0) + r_{t+\tau}(0) - 2r_t(\tau) $$ When the lag (\(\tau\)) is 0, this function will also be zero
